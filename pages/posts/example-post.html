The Six Metrics of Corporate Performance

There are six important metrics of corporate performance:

  • operating and free cash flow margins;
  • operating profit to interest expense;
  • capex to revenue;
  • total debt to operating profit (before depreciation and amortisation); and
  • return on capital

Operating Cash Flow Margin and Free Cash Flow Margin

The operating cash flow margin or OCF margin is defined as aaaaa:

$$ \text{OCF Margin} = \frac{\text{Operating Cash Flow}}{\text{Revenue}}\times100%, $$
where

  • the Operating Cash Flow is the cash generated by the company's core business activities. The figure can be found in the Cash Flow Statement, under "Cash Flow from Operating Activites".
  • The Revenue (or Sales) is the total income from selling goods or services during a specific period. It's usually found on the Income Statement.

A high OCF margin indicates that a company is effective at converting its sales into cash, which is important for paying off debts, investing in growth, and funding operations; a low or decreasing OCF margin indicates issues with cash generation, potentially due to high operating costs, inefficient operations, or problems in working capital management.

The free cash flow margin or FCF margin is defined as:

$$ \text{FCF Margin} = \frac{\text{Free Cash Flow}}{\text{Revenue}}\times100%, $$
where the Free Cash Flow is calculated as
$$\text{FCF} = \text{Operating Cash Flow} - \text{Capital Expendidures (CapEx)}.$$
The Capital Expendidure or CapEx is the money spent on acquiring or maintaining physical assets like property, equipment, or infrastructure. It is found in the Cash Flow Statement.

A high FCF margin is generally a good sign because it indicates that the company is efficiently converting sales into cash that it can use for growth, dividends, or reducing debt; while a a low or decreasing margin might signal that the company is spending a lot on capital expenditures, or it might be struggling to generate cash from operations, which could limit its ability to repay debt, reinvest in its business or return value to shareholders.

If a company has strong OCF but low FCF, this might suggest that it's generating cash from operations but is heavily investing in capital expenditures or facing significant one-time investments (e.g., new facilities or product lines). If FCF exceeds OCF, it could indicate that the company is reducing capital spending or has optimized its capital structure.

Operating Profit to Interest Expense Ratio

The Operating Profit to Interest Expense Ratio is a key indicator of a company's ability to meet its debt obligations from its core business operations. It is calculated as:

$$ \text{Operating Profit to Interest Expense Ratio}=\frac{\text{Operating Profit (EBIT)}}{\text{Interest Expense}}, $$
where

  • the Operating Profit is the Earnings Before Interest and Taxes or EBIT, which represents a company's profit from core operations, excluding interest and taxes (often found in the Income Statement); and
  • the Interest Expense is the cost of the company's debt, including interest paid on loans and bonds. It's usually found in the Income Statement as a separate line item, typically below EBIT.

A high ratio (greater than 1) indicates that the company is generating enough operating profit to comfortably cover its interest expenses. For example, a ratio of 5 means the company can cover its interest payments five times with its operating profit; while a low ratio (closer to or less than 1) indicates that the company may be at risk of struggling to meet its interest payments. If the ratio is 1, it means the company is generating just enough operating profit to cover its interest expenses, with no cushion for unexpected issues.

Capex to Revenue Ratio

The Capital Expendidures to Revenue ratio measures how much a company is spending on capital expenditures (CapEx) relative to its total revenue. It provides insight into how much of a company's revenue is being reinvested into physical assets such as property, plant, equipment, and other long-term investments. It is calculated as:

$$ \text{CapEx to Revenue Ratio} =\frac{\text{Capital Expendidures (CapEx)}}{\text{Total Revenue}}\times 100 $$
A higher ratio often indicates growth or expansion, while a lower ratio suggests more stability or maturity, possibly in industries that don't require significant investment in physical assets.

Total Debt to Operating Profit (Before Depreciation and Amortization)

The Total Debt to Operating Profit (Before Depreciation and Amortization) ratio, often abbreviated as Total Debt to ODBA or Total Debt to EBITDA is calculated as follows:

$$ \text{Total Debt to ODBA}=\frac{\text{Total Debt}}{\text{Operating Profit (Before Depreciation and Amortization)}}, $$
where

  • the Total Debt refers to the sum of both short-term and long-term debt (found on the Balance Sheet); and
  • the Operating Profit Before Depreciation and Amortization (ODBA), also known as EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation and Amortization), represent's a company's earnings from its core business operations before accounting for interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization. The figure is often found on the Income Statement. If not listed, then it can be calculated from:
    $$ \text{EBITDA}=\text{Operating Income (EBIT)}+\text{Depreciation}+\text{Amortization} $$

A higher ratio suggests that the company is more leveraged, meaning it has more debt relative to its earnings from core operations. This could indicate higher financial risk, especially if operating profits fluctuate or decline. A lower ratio suggests the company is less reliant on debt and may have a stronger ability to weather downturns, as it does not need to allocate as much of its operating profit to servicing debt.

The Return on Capital (ROC)

The Return on Capital (ROC) measures the profitability of a company relative to the total capital invested in the business. It is used to assess how efficiently a company is utilizing its capital (both equity and debt) to generate profits. The basic formula for ROC is:

$$ \text{ROC}=\frac{\text{Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT)}}{\text{Total Capital}}. $$
where

  • $\text{NOPAT}=\text{Operating Income (EBIT)}\times(1-\text{Tax Rate})$ represents the profit generated from core business operations, excluding effects of financing and non-operating activities; and
  • the Total Capital is the total amount of capital invested in the business, which can be calculated as the sum of equity capital and debt capital. Alternatively, Total Capital can be the sum of the net working capital (current assets minus current liabilities) and long-term debt.
